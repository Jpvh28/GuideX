How we actually carry fixed-price delivery risk

Fixed price only works if the scope, milestones and acceptance are engineered up front. A look inside how we structure engagements so the risk sits with us, not you.

The GuideX PracticeArchitect-led ServiceNow advisory

"Fixed price" is easy to put on a proposal and hard to stand behind. It only works if the scope, the milestones and the acceptance criteria are engineered before a dollar figure is written. Done properly, the risk sits with us. Done as a headline number over vague scope, it sits with you — as a stream of change requests.

Fixed price is a scoping discipline, not a pricing trick

The number is the last thing we produce, not the first. Before it exists we've built a requirements traceability matrix, defined what "done" means for each deliverable, and named the acceptance test that closes each milestone. A fixed price without that scaffolding is a guess that becomes your problem the moment reality diverges from it.

If the scope isn't engineered, the fixed price is just a starting point for change requests.

How we structure the risk onto us

  • Traceability: every requirement maps to a deliverable and an acceptance test. No orphan scope.
  • Milestone-based acceptance: payment tracks demonstrable, tested outcomes — not effort or time elapsed.
  • Explicit change control: in-scope is defined tightly enough that out-of-scope is obvious and priced separately, so neither side argues about it mid-flight.
  • We carry execution risk: if delivery takes longer than we estimated within agreed scope, that's our cost — not a variation on your invoice.
Use case · CMDB upliftment

A number that held because "done" was written down first

A CMDB upliftment we scoped as a fixed-price engagement covered specific CI classes, named integration sources, and a defined set of acceptance tests per epic. Because "done" was documented per milestone before we started, the client's finance function had genuine cost certainty and their delivery lead spent zero time arbitrating scope. Where new requirements emerged — and they always do — they went through change control as clearly priced additions, not disputes. The engagement closed on the committed number.

The trade-off, stated plainly

Fixed price costs more up front in scoping rigour, and gives up a little mid-stream flexibility, compared with time-and-materials. That's the deal: you trade some agility for cost certainty and transferred risk. For defined uplift and implementation work, it's usually the right trade. For genuinely open-ended discovery, it isn't — and we'll tell you so rather than dress it up.

What to take away

  • The price is the output of scoping, never the input.
  • Requirements → deliverables → acceptance tests, all traceable.
  • Milestones pay on tested outcomes, not effort.
  • Tight in-scope makes out-of-scope obvious — that's what protects both sides.
